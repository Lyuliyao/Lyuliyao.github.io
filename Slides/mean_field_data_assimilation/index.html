Multiscale Nudge

Cross-Scale Data Assimilation for Mean-Field Dynamics

Liyao Lyu
Department of Mathematics
University of California, Los Angeles
Joint work with Hayden Schaeffer and Xinyue Yu
Today I'll present Multiscale Nudge — a framework for injecting macroscopic observations back into microscopic mean-field dynamics. Joint work with Hayden Schaeffer and Xinyue Yu.

Motivation: a scale mismatch in data assimilation

  • Many-body systems (fluids, plasmas, flocks, cells) admit a mean-field limit — a McKean–Vlasov SDE.
  • In practice, the true drift is unknown; only an approximate model is available.
  • Model bias + initial error ⇒ forecast drifts over time.
  • Observations are typically macroscopic: density fields, unlabeled particle locations, not full microscopic state.
  • Classical EnKF / 4D-Var assume same-scale observation — breaks down here.
Goal. Add a feedback (nudging) term to the approximate mean-field SDE so the model distribution stays consistent with macroscopic observations.
The key pain point: microscopic model, macroscopic data. Standard filters weren't designed for this.

True vs. model dynamics (McKean–Vlasov)

True process \(\bX_t\in\Rd\): \[ \intd \bX_t = \btrue(\bX_t,\mu_t)\,\intd t + \Sigma\,\intd\bW_t,\quad \mu_t=\Law(\bX_t). \]
Approximate model \(\bY_t\) (drift known only approximately): \[ \intd \bY_t = \bmodel(\bY_t,\nu_t)\,\intd t + \Sigma\,\intd\bW_t,\quad \nu_t=\Law(\bY_t). \]
Model bias: \( \mathbf{R}(\bx,\mu) := \bmodel(\bx,\mu) - \btrue(\bx,\mu). \)
Sznitman '91 (propagation of chaos); Carmona–Delarue (mean-field lectures).
We keep the diffusion coefficient shared so the noise channel is not the source of error — bias is in the drift.

What do we actually observe?

Let \(K_h(\bx)=h^{-d}K(\bx/h)\) be a smoothing kernel (e.g.\ Gaussian), bandwidth \(h\).

Case A — pixel / density observation \[ \muobs_t(\bz) = (K_h*\mu_t)(\bz). \] Satellite imagery, reconstructed densities.
Case B — unlabeled particle locations \[ \muobs_t(\bz) = \tfrac{1}{M}\sum_{k=1}^{M} K_h(\bz-\bX_t^{\mathrm{obs},k,M}). \] Tracking with dropouts, no index matching.
Both cases enter the same assimilation framework through the same measure \(\muobs\).
The punchline: the assimilated dynamics depends on \mu^{obs} only as a measure — so both observation modalities plug into the same formula.

Observation misfit functional

Quadratic observation-misfit functional (smoothed at observation scale \(h\)):

\[ J_{\mathrm{nud},h}(\nu,\muobs) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\int_{\Rd} \big|(K_h*\nu)(\bx)-\muobs(\bx)\big|^2\,\intd\bx . \]
First variation (Fréchet derivative w.r.t.\ the measure \(\nu\)): \[ \frac{\delta J_{\mathrm{nud},h}}{\delta \nu}(\bx) \;=\; \big(\widetilde K_h*\nu - K_h*\muobs\big)(\bx), \qquad \widetilde K_h := K_h*K_h. \]
Smoothing by \(K_h\) matches the observation scale and restores enough regularity for well-posedness.
K_h smoothing is what lets the nudging term be well-defined when nu is only a measure.

Nudging as a Wasserstein gradient flow

In Wasserstein geometry, steepest descent of \(J_{\mathrm{nud},h}\) has velocity field \(-\nabla\,\delta J/\delta\nu\).

Nudging velocity (intensity \(\lambda>0\)): \[ \bu_{\mathrm{nud}}(\bz,\nu,\muobs) \;=\; -\lambda\,\nabla_\bz\!\left(\widetilde K_h*\nu - K_h*\muobs\right)(\bz). \]
Structure-preserving. If \(\bmodel=-\nabla\frac{\delta F}{\delta\nu}\) and \(\Sigma=\sqrt{2\beta}\,\mathbf I\), the assimilated system is itself a Wasserstein gradient flow of \[ \mathcal F_t(\nu) \;=\; F(\nu) + \lambda\,J_{\mathrm{nud},h}(\nu,\muobs_t) + \beta\,\mathrm{Ent}(\nu). \]
The key structural result: nudging doesn't break the gradient-flow structure — it just adds a time-dependent potential tied to observations.

Assimilated dynamics

Regularized assimilated SDE \[ \begin{aligned} \intd \bZ_t \;=\;& \bmodel(\bZ_t,\nu_t)\,\intd t + \Sigma\,\intd\bW_t \\ & -\,\lambda\,\nabla\!\left(\widetilde K_h*\nu_t - K_h*\muobs_t\right)(\bZ_t)\,\intd t. \end{aligned} \]
Nonlinear Fokker–Planck for \(\nu_t=\Law(\bZ_t)\), with \(A=\Sigma\Sigma^\top,\ r=K_h*\nu_t-\muobs_t\): \[ \begin{aligned} \partial_t\nu_t \;=\;& -\nabla\!\cdot(\nu_t\,\bmodel) + \nabla\!\cdot(A\nabla\nu_t) \\ & +\,\lambda\,\nabla\!\cdot\!\big(\nu_t\,\nabla(K_h*r)\big). \end{aligned} \]

Proposition (Well-posedness)

Under Lipschitz \(\bmodel\) in \((\bx, W_2)\) and Lipschitz \(\nabla\widetilde K_h\), for any \(T>0\) and \(\bZ_0\sim \mathcal P_2(\Rd)\), the assimilated SDE has a unique strong solution on \([0,T]\); its law solves the FPK equation above.
Well-posedness uses the Carmona–Delarue framework: joint Lipschitz of drift in state and Wasserstein.

Interacting particle approximation

Empirical measure \(\nu^N_t=\tfrac1N\sum_j \delta_{\bZ^{j,N}_t}\). Particle dynamics (unlabeled observations):

\[ \begin{aligned} \intd\bZ^{i,N}_t \;=\;& \bmodel(\bZ^{i,N}_t,\nu^N_t)\,\intd t + \Sigma\,\intd\bW^i_t \\ & -\,\lambda\,\Bigl[\,\tfrac1N\!\sum_{j=1}^N\!\nabla\widetilde K_h(\bZ^{i,N}_t{-}\bZ^{j,N}_t) \\ & \qquad\quad -\, \tfrac1M\!\sum_{k=1}^M\!\nabla\widetilde K_h(\bZ^{i,N}_t{-}\bX_t^{\mathrm{obs},k,M})\,\Bigr]\,\intd t. \end{aligned} \]

Proposition (Mean-field convergence / Propagation of chaos)

Assume well-posedness + \(\|\nabla\widetilde K_h\|_\infty<\infty\), \(f_0\)-chaotic initial data. Then \[ \lim_{N\to\infty} W_2\!\left(f^{1,N}_{[0,T]},\,f_{[0,T]}\right)=0. \]
Proof: synchronous coupling, Itô + Grönwall, with a \(1/N\) decorrelation term from the i.i.d.\ copies. (Sznitman; Chaintron–Diez.)

Main result — assumptions

\(\Omega=\mathbb T^d\); \(A=\Sigma\Sigma^\top\) SPD with smallest eigenvalue \(\kappa>0\). Error \(e_t:=\nu_t-\mu_t\), energy \(V(t):=\tfrac12\|e_t\|_{L^2}^2\).

  • (C1) \(L^2\)-Lipschitz model drift: \(\|\bmodel(\cdot,\mu)-\bmodel(\cdot,\nu)\|_{L^2}\le L_\mu\|\mu-\nu\|_{L^2}\).
  • (C2) Density bounds: \(\|\mu_t\|_{L^\infty}\le\bar\rho,\ \nu_t\ge\underline\nu>0\).
  • (C3) Zero-mean initial error: \(\int_\Omega(\nu_0-\mu_0)\,\intd x=0\).
  • (C4) Kernel approximation: \(\|\nabla e_t - \nabla(\widetilde K_h*e_t)\|_{L^2}\le \delta(h)\|\nabla e_t\|_{L^2}\), with \(\delta(h)\to 0\) as \(h\to 0\).
  • (C5) Observation model: \(\muobs_t = K_h*\mu_t\) (no observation noise).

Definitions. \(C_{\mathrm{adv}}:=B_{\mathrm{model}}+\bar\rho\,L_\mu\) (advection instability); \(\Delta:=\sup_t\|\bmodel(\cdot,\mu_t)-\btrue(\cdot,\mu_t)\|_{L^\infty}\) (model bias); \(C_P\) — Poincaré constant on zero-mean functions.

(C5) is the key — observation is the smoothed law, not the law itself. Δ measures uniform model error.

Main result — conclusion

Theorem (\(L^2\) decay under kernel-regularised nudging)

If

\[ \delta(h)\le \frac{\underline\nu}{2\bar\rho}, \qquad \lambda \;>\; \frac{2}{\underline\nu}\!\left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{adv}}^2 C_P}{\kappa}-\kappa\right), \]

then there exists \(\alpha>0\) such that

\[ \tfrac{\intd}{\intd t}V(t)\le -\alpha V(t)+\tfrac{\bar\rho^{\,2}}{\kappa}\Delta^2, \qquad V(t)\le e^{-\alpha t}V(0)+\tfrac{\bar\rho^{\,2}}{\alpha\kappa}\Delta^2. \]
Interpretation. Exponential \(L^2\) contraction to a bias floor \(\propto \Delta^2\) set by model misspecification.
Larger λ beats advective instability via Poincaré; smaller h shrinks the smoothing error δ(h).

Linear 1D benchmark — model & ground truth

Particle system (shared form; \(m_t=\tfrac1N\sum_i X_t^i\) is the empirical mean): \[ \intd X_t^i = -a\bigl(X_t^i - m_t\bigr)\,\intd t + \intd W_t^i. \]
Ground truth
  • \(a_{\mathrm{true}}=1\)
  • \(X_0^i\sim\mathcal N(0,0.5)\)
  • Euler–Maruyama, \(\Delta t = 0.01\), \(T=1\)
Biased forecast model
  • Same equation, wrong interaction \(a\in\{0.5,2,5\}\)
  • \(a<1\): under-interacting (too diffuse)
  • \(a>1\): over-interacting (too concentrated)
Observation: smoothed density \(K_h*\mu_t\) with bandwidth \(h=0.5\) (Gaussian kernel).
Same functional form on both sides — the only source of bias is a mis-specified interaction coefficient a. Observations are density only, not particle positions.

Kinetic setting — Vlasov–Poisson

Phase-space density \(f(t,\bx,\bv)\), self-consistent Poisson coupling:

\[ \partial_t f + \bv\!\cdot\!\nabla_\bx f + E\!\cdot\!\nabla_\bv f \;=\; 0, \] \[ E = -\nabla_\bx\phi,\qquad -\Delta\phi = \rho - 1,\qquad \rho(t,\bx)=\textstyle\int f(t,\bx,\bv)\,\intd\bv. \]
Observation. The smoothed phase-space density \(\muobs_t = K_h * f_t\) on \((\bx,\bv)\) — not just the marginal \(\rho(t,\bx)\).

Model is exact; the only discrepancy comes from imperfect initialization.

Particle-in-cell view: ODE for \((X^i,V^i)\)

Sample \(N\) characteristics of \(f\). Each particle obeys a deterministic ODE driven by the self-consistent field:

\[ \begin{aligned} \intd X_t^i &= V_t^i\,\intd t,\\ \intd V_t^i &= E(X_t^i,t)\,\intd t, \end{aligned} \qquad E = -\nabla_x \phi. \]
Self-consistent coupling via the empirical density: \[ -\Delta\phi(x,t) = \rho^N(x,t) - 1, \qquad \rho^N(x,t) = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\delta(x-X_t^i). \]
As \(N\to\infty\), the empirical phase-space measure \(f^N_t = \tfrac1N\sum_i\delta_{(X^i_t,V^i_t)}\) converges to the Vlasov–Poisson solution \(f_t\). Nudging acts on \((X^i,V^i)\) via the Wasserstein gradient of \(K_h*f^N - K_h*f\).
This is the bridge: the Vlasov PDE on the previous slide is the mean-field limit of these particle ODEs. The nudging mechanism applies directly to the particle level.

Landau damping

Maxwellian \(f_{M,\sigma}(v)=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}e^{-v^2/(2\sigma^2)}\); \(x\in[0,4\pi],\ v\in[-6,6]\).

Truth: \(f(x,v,0)\propto f_{M,1}(v)\bigl(1+\varepsilon\cos(\kappa x)\bigr)\),
\(\varepsilon=0.5,\ \kappa=\tfrac12\).
Biased model: same dynamics, but initialized from the unperturbed Maxwellian (\(\varepsilon=0\)) — no initial density modulation.

Two-stream instability — initial conditions

Truth — bimodal velocity distribution with weak spatial perturbation: \[ f(x,v,0)\propto \tfrac12\bigl(f_{M,\sigma}(v-u_0)+f_{M,\sigma}(v+u_0)\bigr)\bigl(1+\varepsilon\cos(\kappa x)\bigr). \] \(\sigma=0.2,\ u_0=1,\ \varepsilon=0.01,\ \kappa=0.5\).
Biased model — single Maxwellian, same perturbation: \[ f(x,v,0)\propto f_{M,1}(v)\bigl(1+\varepsilon\cos(\kappa x)\bigr). \] Misses the underlying two-stream structure entirely.
The bias here is structural — single-Maxwellian initialization cannot generate the bimodal phase-space dynamics.
